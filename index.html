EQUIPMENT GUIDE

🧯 Fire extinguisher colours and uses

The cylinder body is normally red; the coloured band identifies the contents.

🚨
Life safety comes first

Raise the alarm and evacuate. Only attempt a very small fire if trained, the correct extinguisher is available and you have a clear escape route behind you.

💧

Water

Red identification band
Class A

Wood, paper, cardboard, textiles and many ordinary solid combustibles.

Do not use on live electrical equipment, flammable liquids, gases or cooking oils.
🫧

Foam

Cream identification band
Class AClass B

Solid materials and many flammable-liquid fires such as petrol or paint.

Do not use on cooking oils. Check the extinguisher marking before use near electrical equipment.
🌫️

Dry powder

Blue identification band
Class AClass BClass C

Multipurpose powder can cover solids, flammable liquids and flammable gases.

Powder can reduce visibility and affect breathing; specialist advice is important, particularly indoors.
☁️

Carbon dioxide

Black identification band
Class BElectrical

Flammable liquids and electrical equipment such as computers and switchgear.

Does not cool well, so re-ignition is possible. The horn can become extremely cold.
🍳

Wet chemical

Yellow identification band
Class FClass A*

Cooking oils and fats, particularly deep-fat fryers. Some are also rated for Class A.

Never use water on burning cooking oil. Follow the extinguisher label and training.

PATROL POCKET GUIDES

Common inspection subjects

Open a card for practical warning signs.

🚪➡️

Means of escape

Routes must remain clear, visible and usable without delay.

What to look for
  • Locked or obstructed exits
  • Storage or trip hazards in routes
  • Missing or confusing signs
  • Unsafe conditions outside final exits
🚪

Fire doors

Fire doors protect escape routes and limit smoke and fire spread.

What to look for
  • Wedged or tied open doors
  • Failed self-closers
  • Damage, excessive gaps or missing seals
  • Unapproved holes or alterations
🔔

Detection and alarms

The system should be available, visible and free from obvious faults.

What to look for
  • Fault, disablement or isolation indicators
  • Covered or damaged detectors
  • Blocked manual call points
  • Areas where alarms may not be heard or seen
💡

Emergency lighting

Escape routes need illumination if normal lighting fails.

What to look for
  • Damaged or missing units
  • No healthy indicator light
  • Dark stairs, changes of direction or exits
  • Missing test and maintenance records
🔋

Lithium-ion batteries

Charging and storage need control, separation and supervision.

What to look for
  • Damaged, swollen or unusually hot batteries
  • Unapproved chargers or trailing leads
  • Charging in escape routes
  • Combustibles stored around charging areas
🧹

Housekeeping

Good housekeeping removes fuel and keeps safety equipment accessible.

What to look for
  • Waste and cardboard accumulation
  • Storage against heaters or switchgear
  • Blocked extinguishers or alarm points
  • Poor control of external bins and arson risk
🔎

No matching guide

Try a simpler word such as “door”, “alarm” or “extinguisher”.
